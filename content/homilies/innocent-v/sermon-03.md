## Original Text

{{< section "1" >}}

Benedicat nos Deus, et metuant eum omnes fines terrae. (Ps. 66. 6.)

Rabbi, scimus, quia a Deo venisti magister. Haec dixit Nicodemus in Evangelio hodierno ad Christum. (Jo. 3. 2.) Ante adventum Christi multi fuerunt magistri: in arte Dialectica Aristoteles, et sectatores; multi in arte medicinae: Hippocrates et similiter. Multi in arte rethorica, legis et scientiarum humanarum doctores, Labeo, Ulpianus etc. Sed nullus fuerat magister Theologiae vel scientiae divinae. Ideo conqueritur Osea propheta: (4. 1.) «non est veritas» scilicet in ore, «non est misericordia» scilicet in corde, «non est scientia Domini in terra» scilicet in opere. Tunc sculptilia, lapides et ligna adorabant: haec necessitate, divina misericordia, compulsa, a se ipsa deciderunt. Misit filium suum in terra verum magistrum theologiae. Matth. (23. 8.) «nolite vocari Rabbi, idest magistri, quia unus est magister vester» in coelis Christus: quia ille solus perfectus magister.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "2" >}}

Hunc verum magistrum verissime tria signa declararunt, et ideo scimus quia a Deo venisti magister. Primum signum, divina miracula plena admiratione, iuxta illud: «vidimus miracula illius.» Secundum signum, doctrina eius, et divina verba, iuxta illud: «Sic numquam locutus fuit homo ut ipse.» (Jo. 7. 46.) Tertium signum fuit conversationis operatio, iuxta illud: «quis ex vobis arguet me de peccato?» (Jo. 8. 46.)

Primum ergo signum fuerunt miracula exteriora virtutum, et ideo (1.° Jo. 5. 7.): «tres sunt, qui testimonium dant in coelo: Pater, et Filius, et Spiritus Sanctus. Et tres sunt, qui testimonium dant in terra: Spiritus, aqua, et sanguis.» Et huiusmodi tres sunt secundum glomerationem, Pater in potentia miraculorum; Verbum vel Filius in Sapientia documentorum; Spiritus Sanctus in virtute et gratia exemplorum. Ipse solus secreta divina novit: innascibilitate Patris, Filii generatione, Spiritus Sancti processione. Hic dicit (Jo. 3. 12.): «si terrena dixi vobis, et non creditis; quomodo, si dixero vobis coelestia, credetis?»

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "3" >}}

In Trinitate supercoelesti periculosissimum est errare, laboriosum investigare, et fructuosum invenire. Augustinus de Trinit.: De re summa cum modestia accedendum est, quia nec periculosius alicubi erratur, scilicet quia fundamentum est fidei nostrae; nihil laboriosius quaeritur quam scilicet quod humana ratio non pertingit, nihil fructuosius reperitur iuxta illud (Jo. 17. 3.): «haec est vita aeterna ut cognoscant te solum Deum etc.» Hunc magistrum invocemus, ut hujus secreti thesaurum nobis aperiat.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "4" >}}

Benedicat nos Deus etc. Sancta et beatissima Trinitas est principium et finis omnium. Apoc. (1. 8.) «ego sum alpha et omega» idest, principium et finis. Ideo haec festivitas sanctae et individuae Trinitatis principium et finis est aliarum omnium festivitatum. In aliis festivitatibus laudatur Deus in Sanctis, vel in incarnatione sua, vel in pertinentibus ad suam incarnationem; hic laudatur in natura suae Trinitatis. Ps. (150. 1.) «laudate Dominum in Sanctis eius, laudate eum in firmamento virtutis eius.»

In aliis festivitatibus contemplatur in montibus aeternis; in hac contemplatur in aquila et rota sui splendoris, scilicet, Patris et Filii et Spiritus Sancti, qui solus est aeternus: solus in essentia, trinus in personis, proprietatibus et relationibus, et his tribus tot modis illuminat: potentia Patris, sapientia Filii, bonitate Spiritus Sancti. Ecclesiastici (43. 4.): «Sol tripliciter exurens montes» quod ostendit David in verbis praemissis: Benedicat nos Deus, quibus verbis tria insinuat per triplicem Dei receptionem.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "5" >}}

Primo quid credendum et tenendum in fide, quia credere debemus Trinitatem personarum in unitate essentiae in tertia repetitione eiusdem nominis, et in singulari denominatione unitatem essentiae, quia non tres dii. Secundo quid ab ea petendum, quam benedictio gratiae in praesenti et gloriae in futuro? Benedicat nos Deus. Tertio ostendit qualiter ei serviendum a nobis, quia cum reverentia et timore: metuant eum omnes fines terrae.

Primum pertinet ad fidei sacramenta, et rectam professionem. Secundum ad humilem supplicationem. Tertium ad coelestem conversationem.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "6" >}}

Credendum igitur illud sine quo nullus est vere Christianus, scilicet Trinitatem personarum in unitate substantiae. Glossa praedicti Ps. benedicat, in fine, Deus misereatur nostri. Unitas essentiae demonstratur in singularitate pronominis, et Trinitas personarum in eiusdem nominis trina repetitione. Singularitas praenominis cum dicit: et metuant eum.

Credamus ergo Patrem Deum, Filium Deum, Spiritum Sanctum Deum, et tamen non tres dii; sed unus Deus ut in symbolo Athanasii. Boethius: essentia continet unitatem, relatio multiplicat Trinitatem. Augustinus: non enim alius Pater, alius Filius, alius Spiritus Sanctus.

Si tu quaeras quomodo Nicodemus, qui nocte venit ad Jesum, idest si tu in nocte ignorantiae existens, et in his dubitans quaeras aliquid, respondebo cum Hilario lib. secundo de Trinitate: ego nescio, non requiro, et consolabor: mecum archangeli nesciunt; angeli non audierunt, saecula non tenent: Propheta non scit, Apostolus non interrogavit, Filius non edidit. Huius enim rei perfecta cognitio et comprehensio excedit rationem philosophicam, revelationem propheticam, traditionem apostolicam, speculationem angelicam, sicut dicitur Isaiae (6. 1.): «vidi Dominum sedentem super solium excelsum et elevatum», scilicet super intellectum et rationem et elevationem et speculationem, «et plena erat omnis terra maiestate eius, et ea quae sub ipso erant replebant templum.» Apostolus (1. Tim. 6. 16.): «lucem inhabitat inaccessibilem etc.» Ideo non potest ratione comprehendi.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "7" >}}

In tamen secundum Jo. Damascenum non reliquit nos in omnimoda ignorantia, sed viam quadrupliciter nobis reliquit ad sui in Trinitate cognitionem. Primo vestigium totius creaturae. Secundo speculum imaginis divinae. Tertio eloquium Sacrae Scripturae. Quarto, documentum et professionem universalis Ecclesiae.

Primo ergo reliquit ad cognoscendam Trinitatem personarum in unitate essentiae vestigium totius creaturae. Jo. Damascenus: quocumque te vertis, quasi quibusdam vestigiis tibi loquitur Deus, in quibus manifestatur potentia in Trinitate, sapientia in dispositione, bonitas in gubernatione. Ad Rom. (1. 20.) «invisibilia Dei, per ea quae sunt, intellecta conspiciuntur»; quia ex ipso in creatione et per ipsum in gubernatione, et in ipso fuerunt reductione omnia. Hoc ostendit rerum creatarum magnitudo, pulcritudo et venustas. Hoc ostendit Trinitas potentiarum, memoriae, intelligentiae et voluntatis in identitate substantiae. Magnitudo rerum ostendit potentiam Patris, pulcritudo Sapientiam Filii, usus earum Bonitatem Spiritus Sancti. Hoc non pertingit humana ratio complete. Job. (11. 7.): «forsitan vestigia Dei comprehendes, et usque ad perfectionem pervenies?» quasi dicat, non. Tende ergo ad laudem cuius ex [lacuna] bonitate.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "8" >}}

Secunda via est speculum imaginis divinae. In speculo resultat imago intuentis. Speculum autem huius imaginis est intellectus humanus, sive homo factus ad imaginem Dei. Sic Deus in mentibus nostris impressit imaginem Trinitatis. Genes. (1. 26.): «faciamus hominem ad imaginem et similitudinem nostram.» Glossa Augustini ibidem: mens imago Dei est, quia in unitate substantiae potest memorari intelligere et velle. Apostolus (1.a Cor. 13. 12.): «nunc videmus per speculum in aenigmate etc.» Sed sicut in speculo materiali non resultat imago nisi sit mundissimum, sic oportet quod humanus intellectus sit mundissimus et immunis a peccato, ut sufficienter esse Trinitas in ipso conspiciatur. Unde in Evangelio (Matt. 5. 8.): «beati mundo corde, quoniam ipsi Deum videbunt.» Sap. vii. (26.): «candor est lucis aeternae, et speculum sine macula.»

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "9" >}}

Tertia via qua Trinitas indagatur sunt eloquia sacrae Scripturae. Ps. (33. 6.): «verbo Domini coeli firmati sunt, et spiritu oris eius omnis virtus eorum.»

Propheta hic ostendit manifeste Trinitatem personarum per Verbum Filium, per Dominum Patrem, et Spiritum Sanctum. Hoc idem ostendit scriptura evangelica Marc. (16. 15.): «euntes praedicate Evangelium omni creaturae, baptisantes eos in nomine Patris, et Filii, et Spiritus Sancti.» Idem ostendit Scriptura Apostolica in Canonica Joannis (5. 7.): «tres sunt qui testimonium dant in coelo. Pater et Filius et Spiritus Sanctus, et hi tres unum sunt.»

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "10" >}}

Quarto, per documentum et professionem universalis Ecclesiae et sanctorum Patrum manifestatur Trinitas personarum in unitate essentiae. Lucae (13. 21.): «Simile est regnum coelorum fermento panis, quod fermentavit mulier», idest universalis Ecclesia quae dat documenta de articulis fidei secundum illud Jo. (1.a Jo. 5. 7.): «tres sunt qui testimonium dant in terra etc.» Hoc dicit concors praedicatio doctorum apostolicorum, iuxta illud: «non enim vos estis qui loquimini, sed Spiritus Patris vestri qui loquitur in vobis.» Aqua scilicet significat sacramentum, sanguis confessio martyrum qui pro Christo sanguinem suum fuderunt.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "11" >}}

In via prima vestigiis deviaverunt gentiles philosophi, ut dicitur ad Romanos (1. 21.): quia creaturas curiosissime investigaverunt, et ut in his debebant creatorem non glorificaverunt, aut gratias non egerunt, quod conqueritur Baruch (3. 23.): «filii Agar, idest gentiles, qui exquisierunt scientiam, negotiatores Merrhae et Theman, idest advocati, et fabulatores eorum, idest sophistae, inquisitores prudentiae, viam sapientiae nescierunt, nec voluerunt scire dona gratiae Spiritus Sancti», et ideo magi Pharaonis defecerunt in tertio signo.

Oh quanti sic hodie errant? Sine veritate, sine humilitate superbiendo, sine charitate laborando, scilicet ut ditentur addiscunt. Sic errat, sine Spiritu Sancto addiscit qui in addiscendo superbit, nec quaerit aedificationem sui et aliorum in scientia, sed lucrum temporale. Ps. (70. 15.) secundum aliam translationem: introibo in potentias Domini, quoniam non cognovi littératuram, idest in negatione.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "12" >}}

In secunda via, idest speculo imaginis, deviaverunt Pagani iuxta illud (Rom. 1. 24.): obscuratum est cor eorum quia in peccatis ambulant. Commutaverunt imaginem incorruptibilem in corruptibilia, et lapides adoraverunt. Ps. (48. 20.): «computati sunt iumentis insipientibus, et similes facti sunt illis.»

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "13" >}}

In tertia via, scilicet eloquio Scripturae, erraverunt carnales Judaei, quibus Dominus dixit in Matt. (22. 29.): «erratis nescientes Scripturas.» Cor. (2.a Cor. 3. 15.): «usque hodie, dum legitur Moyses, velamen est positum super caput eorum.»

In quarta via, scilicet documento universalis Ecclesiae, erraverunt haeretici, quod valde horrendum, quia rustici et ignari, ut haeretici sensum suum proferre praesumunt sanctis doctoribus et expositoribus Sanctorum Patrum, cum cunctae sacrae Scripturae eodem Spiritu traditae fuerunt quo expositae. Ps. (17. 46.): «filii alieni mentiti sunt mihi» idest haeretici alienati et separati ab Ecclesia «inveterati sunt et claudicaverunt a semitis suis.» Baruch (3. 19.): «excaecati sunt et ad inferos descenderunt», quos sequi renuamus, consequamur matrem nostram Ecclesiam.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "14" >}}

Augustinus de fide ad Petrum: firmissime tene et indubitanter adstrue, scilicet quantum ad cordis, oris, operis, conversationis et fidei unitatem Patris, et Filii et Spiritus Sancti unum esse Deum. 2. Tim. (4. 7. 8.): «bonum certamen certavi, fidem servavi, de reliquo reposita est mihi corona iustitiae, quam mihi reddet iustus iudex» ad quam nos perducat etc.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "15" >}}

Collatio fratris de Tarantasia de Trinitate.

Benedicat nos etc. Verba sunt Ps. (66. 6.) in quibus possumus videre primo quid credendum, quia Trinitatem. In his enim verbis tangitur persona Patris: Deus; persona Filii: Deus noster; persona Spiritus Sancti: Benedicat nos Deus. Sciendum quid videre possumus in verbis istis, et quid petere debeamus, quoniam benedictionem gratiae et gloriae. Nota a quo, quomodo, et quae benedictio, et quarto quibus mediis sit petenda.

A Deo petenda est. Minor a maiore benedicitur, et nos nihil sumus respectu illius, ideo ab ipso benedictionem petere debemus.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "16" >}}

Legimus quatuor benedictiones datas. Legimus in Genesi (49. 28.) quod Jacob benedixit filiis suis, et benedixit singulis benedictionibus propriis. Legimus benedictionem pontificalem. (Levitic. 9. 22.) «Extendens manum suam Aaron benedixit eis.» Legimus benedictionem iudicialem, (Deut. 33.) qua benedixit Moyses in Deuteronomio filiis Israel. Legimus benedictionem regiam qua benedixit Salomon. (3.a Reg. 8. 55.) «Unde stetit rex, et benedixit filiis Israel voce magna.»

Benedictione pontificali, regia, iudiciali benedixit Dominus. De pontificali in Levitico (20. 8.): «Ego Dominus qui sanctifico.» De iudiciali Ps. (24. 9.): «docebit nos Dominus vias suas.» Benedictione regia nos benedicit, quia ipse rex est. Ps. (79. 2.): «qui regis Israel, intende: qui deducis etc.» Ab ipso ergo petamus benedictionem.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "17" >}}

Et quomodo? Solent enim homines suo episcopo obviam exire, capulia deponere, benedictionem instanter petere, et capita inclinare. Sic et nos Trinitati ad nos per officium Ecclesiae venienti debemus exire obviam carnalia transeundo. «Ecce sponsus venit, exite obviam ei» per depositionem carnalitatum. Deponamus capulia terrenarum cogitationum, phantasmata talium cogitationum removendo. Vir non debet velare caput pravis phantasmatibus; et hoc ut possit clare respicere coelestia et contemplari. Item hic nos humiliando, quia superbis Deus resistit, humilibus etc. secundum B. Jacobum (4. 6.). Item debemus petere instanter per orationem. Ps. (27. 8.): «Salvum fac populum tuum, Domine, et benedic haereditati tuae.» Et alibi: «Benedicat nos Deus etc.»

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "18" >}}

Sed quid est ista benedictio quae est a nobis petenda? Scire debetis quod multiplex est benedictio Domini secundum quod ab ipso egrediuntur quatuor bona. Est benedictio fortuitae, quae consistit in bonis temporalibus. Prov. (10. 22.): «Benedictio Domini divites facit.» Et in Gen. (30. 29. 30.) dicit Jacob ad Laban: «nosti, qualiter tibi servierim, et quomodo benedixit te Deus ad introitum meum.» Istam multi desiderant; istam desiderabat Esau.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "19" >}}

Est benedictio naturae, quae consistit in abundantia bonorum naturalium, scilicet in abundantia boni ingenii, fortitudinis, sanitatis et huiusmodi. Ps. (144. 16.): «aperis tu manum tuam, et imples omne animal benedictione.»

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "20" >}}

Tertia est benedictio gratiae. Eccli. (40. 17.): «gratia Dei sicut Paradisus in benedictionibus», et bene sicut Paradisus, quia in isto est lignum vitae, scilicet amor boni, lignum scientiae: cognitio veri, fluvius dulcis, scilicet pietas proximi. Ibi (Gen. 1. 11.): «et lignum pomiferum», scilicet virtus bene conversandi et operandi. Anima quae habet hanc benedictionem gratiae est Paradisus, unde gratia Dei facit animam isto modo.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "21" >}}

Quarta est benedictio gloriae, quae consistit in abundantia coelestium bonorum: qui in gloria sunt habent benedictionem a coelo, quia vident gloriam bonorum; ab abysso, quia vident vindictam malorum. Paulus ad Eph. (1. 3.): «qui benedixit nos omni benedictione spirituali.» Gen. «ingredere, benedicte Domini, cur foris stas?»

Prima benedictio non est petenda, quia est materia cupiditatis, ideo non est multum curanda, quia est materia superbiae et elationis. Secunda benedictio gratiae est multum desideranda, quia est materia sanctitatis; sed benedictio gloriae maxime est appetenda, quia est materia omnis boni; ibi omnia habentur bona. Unde dicamus: Benedicat nos Deus, Deus noster etc.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "22" >}}

Quarto, quibus mediis haec benedictio sit petenda; et dico ad praesens, quia quatuor. Sunt enim quatuor virtutes quae istam benedictionem obtinent, et qua merentur; quae sunt innocentia, misericordia, obedientia, charitas. De tribus primis quaere in sermone de beato Benedicto in sermonibus Lugdunensibus de festis.

De charitate nota secundum Augustinum quod dilectio nos Deo assimilat, unit et informat.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "23" >}}

Nota etiam quod ista charitate quae ducit ad benedictionem gloriae sunt gradus: 1.° ardens desiderium. 2.° gemitus et suspirium. 3.° divina consolatio. 4.° firma adhaesio, et hic gradus meretur Dei benedictionem, quod figuratum est in lucta Jacob cum angelo. Gen. (32. 26. 29.) ubi dicitur: «non dimittam te nisi benedixeris mihi», et benedixit eum in eodem loco.

{{< /section >}}

## English Translation

{{< section "1" >}}

{{< verse ref="Ps 66:8" >}}May God bless us, and may all the ends of the earth fear him.{{< /verse >}}

{{< verse ref="John 3:2" >}}Rabbi, we know that thou art come a teacher from God.{{< /verse >}} These words were spoken by Nicodemus to Christ in today's Gospel. Before the coming of Christ there were many masters: in the art of Dialectic, Aristotle and his followers; many in the art of medicine, Hippocrates and the like; many in rhetoric and the law and in the learning of human sciences, Labeo, Ulpianus, and others. But there had been no master of Theology or of divine knowledge. Therefore the prophet Osee laments: {{< verse ref="Osee 4:1" >}}There is no truth, and there is no mercy, and there is no knowledge of God in the land.{{< /verse >}} — no truth, that is, in the mouth; no mercy in the heart; no knowledge of the Lord in the land, that is, in deed. Then they worshipped carved images, stones and wood; these, compelled as it were by necessity and by divine mercy, fell away of themselves. He sent his Son into the world as the true master of theology. {{< verse ref="Matt 23:8" >}}Be not you called Rabbi. For one is your master.{{< /verse >}} — that is, be not called masters, for there is one master in heaven, Christ: because he alone is the perfect master.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "2" >}}

Three signs most truly declared this true master, and therefore: *we know that thou art come a teacher from God.* The first sign was divine miracles full of wonder, according to the saying: *we have seen his miracles.* The second sign was his teaching and his divine words, according to the saying: {{< verse ref="John 7:46" >}}Never did man speak like this man.{{< /verse >}} The third sign was the conduct of his life, according to the saying: {{< verse ref="John 8:46" >}}Which of you shall convince me of sin?{{< /verse >}}

The first sign, then, was the outward miracles of his power; and therefore {{< verse ref="1 John 5:7" >}}There are three who give testimony in heaven: the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost. And these three are one. And there are three that give testimony on earth: the spirit, and the water, and the blood.{{< /verse >}} And these three correspond in their grouping thus: the Father in the power of miracles; the Word, or Son, in the Wisdom of teachings; the Holy Ghost in the virtue and grace of examples. He alone knows the divine secrets: the unbegottenness of the Father, the generation of the Son, the procession of the Holy Ghost. He says: {{< verse ref="John 3:12" >}}If I have spoken to you earthly things, and you believe not: how will you believe, if I shall speak to you heavenly things?{{< /verse >}}

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "3" >}}

Concerning the most exalted Trinity, it is most perilous to err, toilsome to enquire, and fruitful to discover. Augustine, in *On the Trinity*, says: One must approach the highest matter with modesty, for nowhere is there greater danger of error — since it is the foundation of our faith — nothing is sought with greater labour than what human reason cannot reach, and nothing more fruitfully found, according to the saying: {{< verse ref="John 17:3" >}}Now this is eternal life: That they may know thee, the only true God.{{< /verse >}} Let us invoke this master, that he may open to us the treasure of this mystery.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "4" >}}

*May God bless us, etc.* The holy and most blessed Trinity is the beginning and end of all things. {{< verse ref="Apoc 1:8" >}}I am Alpha and Omega.{{< /verse >}} — that is, the beginning and the end. Therefore this feast of the holy and undivided Trinity is the beginning and end of all other feasts. In other feasts God is praised in his Saints, or in his Incarnation, or in matters pertaining to his Incarnation; here he is praised in the nature of his Trinity. {{< verse ref="Ps 150:1" >}}Praise ye the Lord in his holy places: praise ye him in the firmament of his power.{{< /verse >}}

In other feasts he is contemplated upon the eternal mountains; in this feast he is contemplated in the eagle and the wheel of his splendour — that is, of the Father and the Son and the Holy Ghost, who alone is eternal: one in essence, threefold in persons, properties, and relations, and by these three he illumines in as many ways: through the power of the Father, the wisdom of the Son, the goodness of the Holy Ghost. Ecclesiasticus: {{< verse ref="Eccli 43:4" >}}A man heating the mountains in three ways with the sun.{{< /verse >}} [Trans. note: the original reads *Sol tripliciter exurens montes*, lit. "the sun scorching the mountains in three ways"; the Douay-Rheims rendering differs somewhat.] This David declares in the words before us — *May God bless us* — in which three things are intimated by a threefold reception of God.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "5" >}}

First, what is to be believed and held in faith: we must believe the Trinity of persons in the unity of essence, expressed in the threefold repetition of the same name and in the unity of essence expressed by the singular form, since there are not three gods. Second, what is to be sought from him: what blessing but the grace of the present and the glory of the future? *May God bless us.* Third, it shows how we are to serve him, namely with reverence and fear: *and may all the ends of the earth fear him.*

The first pertains to the mysteries of faith and right profession. The second pertains to humble supplication. The third pertains to a heavenly manner of life.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "6" >}}

What is to be believed, then, is that without which no one is truly a Christian: the Trinity of persons in the unity of substance. The Gloss on the said Psalm comments on *bless*, at the end, *God have mercy on us.* The unity of essence is shown in the singular form of the pronoun, and the Trinity of persons in the threefold repetition of the same name. The singular pronoun is shown in the words: *and may all fear him.*

Let us therefore believe the Father to be God, the Son to be God, the Holy Ghost to be God, and yet not three gods but one God, as in the Creed of Athanasius. Boethius says: substance contains unity; relation multiplies Trinity. Augustine says: for the Father is not other than the Son, nor the Son other than the Holy Ghost.

If you ask how — as Nicodemus, who came to Jesus by night, that is, if you, dwelling in the night of ignorance and doubting in these things, seek some answer — I will reply with Hilary, in the second book of *On the Trinity*: I do not know, I do not inquire, and yet I shall take comfort: the archangels know it not; the angels have not heard; the ages do not hold it; the Prophet did not perceive it; the Apostle did not question it; the Son himself did not reveal it. For the perfect knowledge and comprehension of this thing exceeds philosophical reason, prophetic revelation, apostolic tradition, and angelic speculation, as it is said in Isaiah: {{< verse ref="Isa 6:1" >}}I saw the Lord sitting upon a throne high and elevated.{{< /verse >}} — that is, above intellect and reason and elevation and speculation — *and his train filled the temple.* The Apostle says: {{< verse ref="1 Tim 6:16" >}}Who only hath immortality, and inhabiteth light inaccessible.{{< /verse >}} Therefore it cannot be comprehended by reason.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "7" >}}

Yet, according to John of Damascus, God has not left us in complete ignorance, but has left us four paths to the knowledge of himself in the Trinity. First, the vestige of the whole creation. Second, the mirror of the divine image. Third, the words of Sacred Scripture. Fourth, the teaching and profession of the universal Church.

First, then, he left us the vestige of the whole creation as a means of knowing the Trinity of persons in the unity of essence. John of Damascus says: wherever you turn, God speaks to you as it were through certain footprints, in which his power is shown in the Trinity, his wisdom in the ordering of things, his goodness in their governance. To the Romans: {{< verse ref="Rom 1:20" >}}For the invisible things of him, from the creation of the world, are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made.{{< /verse >}} For all things are from him in creation, through him in governance, and in him in their return. This is shown by the greatness, beauty, and loveliness of created things. This is shown by the Trinity of powers — memory, intellect, and will — in the identity of substance. The greatness of things shows the power of the Father, their beauty the Wisdom of the Son, the use of them the Goodness of the Holy Ghost. Yet human reason cannot fully attain this. Job: {{< verse ref="Job 11:7" >}}Peradventure thou wilt comprehend the steps of God, and wilt find out the Almighty perfectly?{{< /verse >}} — as if to say: no. Strive therefore toward the praise of him whose [lacuna] goodness. [Trans. note: a word is missing in the manuscript at this point and cannot be restored.]

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "8" >}}

The second way is the mirror of the divine image. In a mirror the image of the one who looks is reflected. Now the mirror of this image is the human intellect, that is, man made in the image of God. Thus God has imprinted the image of the Trinity upon our minds. Genesis: {{< verse ref="Gen 1:26" >}}Let us make man to our image and likeness.{{< /verse >}} Augustine's Gloss on the same passage: the mind is the image of God, because in the unity of substance it can remember, understand, and will. The Apostle says: {{< verse ref="1 Cor 13:12" >}}We see now through a glass in a dark manner.{{< /verse >}} But just as in a material mirror no image is reflected unless the mirror is very clean, so it is necessary that the human intellect be most pure and free from sin, that the Trinity may be sufficiently discerned in it. Hence in the Gospel: {{< verse ref="Matt 5:8" >}}Blessed are the clean of heart: for they shall see God.{{< /verse >}} Wisdom: {{< verse ref="Wis 7:26" >}}For she is the brightness of eternal light, and the unspotted mirror of God's majesty.{{< /verse >}}

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "9" >}}

The third way by which the Trinity is sought out is the words of Sacred Scripture. The Psalm: {{< verse ref="Ps 32:6" >}}By the word of the Lord the heavens were established; and all the power of them by the spirit of his mouth.{{< /verse >}}

The Prophet here manifestly shows the Trinity of persons: the Word for the Son, the Lord for the Father, and the Holy Ghost. The same is shown by the evangelical Scripture: {{< verse ref="Mark 16:15" >}}Go ye into the whole world, and preach the gospel to every creature.{{< /verse >}} — baptising them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost. The same is shown by the Apostolic Scripture in the Epistle of John: {{< verse ref="1 John 5:7" >}}And there are three who give testimony in heaven: the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost. And these three are one.{{< /verse >}}

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "10" >}}

Fourth, the Trinity of persons in the unity of essence is made manifest through the teaching and profession of the universal Church and the holy Fathers. Luke: {{< verse ref="Luke 13:21" >}}It is like to leaven, which a woman took and hid in three measures of meal.{{< /verse >}} — that is, the universal Church, which gives instruction on the articles of faith, according to the saying of John: *there are three who give testimony on earth, etc.* This is declared by the concordant preaching of the apostolic doctors, according to the saying: *For it is not you that speak, but the Spirit of your Father that speaketh in you.* Water signifies the sacrament; blood, the confession of the martyrs who shed their blood for Christ.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "11" >}}

In the first way — that of vestiges — the Gentile philosophers went astray, as is said in Romans: for they investigated created things with the greatest curiosity, yet did not glorify the Creator as they ought, nor give him thanks; of which Baruch complains: the sons of Agar, that is the Gentiles, who have sought out wisdom, the merchants of Merrha and Theman, that is the advocates, and their storytellers, that is the sophists, the searchers after understanding, did not know the way of wisdom, nor did they wish to know the gifts of the grace of the Holy Ghost — and therefore the magicians of Pharaoh failed at the third sign.

How many today go astray in this same manner! — learning in order to grow rich, without truth, without humility, swelling with pride, labouring without charity. So errs, and learns without the Holy Ghost, the one who grows proud in learning and seeks not the edification of himself and others in knowledge, but only temporal gain. The Psalm, according to another translation: *I will enter into the power of the Lord; for I have not known letters* — that is, in denial of them.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "12" >}}

In the second way, that is, the mirror of the image, the Pagans went astray, according to the saying of Romans: their heart was darkened because they walk in sins. They exchanged the incorruptible image for corruptible things, and worshipped stones. The Psalm: *they are compared to senseless beasts, and are become like to them.*

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "13" >}}

In the third way, that of the words of Scripture, the carnal Jews went astray, to whom the Lord said in Matthew: {{< verse ref="Matt 22:29" >}}You err, not knowing the Scriptures.{{< /verse >}} And in the second letter to the Corinthians: *even until this day, when Moses is read, the veil is upon their heart.*

In the fourth way, that of the teaching of the universal Church, the heretics went astray — a most dreadful thing — for the unlearned and ignorant presume, like heretics, to put forward their own sense against the holy doctors and expositors of the Holy Fathers, although all of Sacred Scripture was handed down by the same Spirit by which it was expounded. The Psalm: {{< verse ref="Ps 17:46" >}}The children that are strangers have lied to me.{{< /verse >}} — that is, the heretics, estranged and separated from the Church — *they have halted from their paths.* Baruch: *they were blinded and went down into hell* — let us refuse to follow them, and instead let us follow our mother the Church.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "14" >}}

Augustine, in *On Faith to Peter*, says: hold most firmly and assert without doubt — as regards the unity of heart, mouth, work, conduct, and faith — that the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost are one God. Second Timothy: {{< verse ref="2 Tim 4:7" >}}I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith. As to the rest, there is laid up for me a crown of justice, which the Lord the just judge will render to me.{{< /verse >}} To which may he bring us, etc.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "15" >}}

*A Collation of Brother de Tarentaise on the Trinity.*

[Trans. note: What follows is a supplementary collation appended to the sermon by the manuscript scribe, who drew on other sermon collections of the same author to fill gaps in the text. It takes the same psalm verse as its text and develops a fourfold meditation on blessing.]

*May God bless us, etc.* These are the words of Psalm 66:6, in which we can see first what is to be believed, namely the Trinity. For in these words the person of the Father is touched upon — *God*; the person of the Son — *our God*; the person of the Holy Ghost — *May God bless us.* We should know what we can see in these words and what we ought to ask for, namely the blessing of grace and of glory. Note from whom, in what manner, what blessing is asked, and fourthly, by what means it is to be sought.

It is to be sought from God. The lesser is blessed by the greater, and we are nothing in comparison with him; therefore we ought to seek blessing from him.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "16" >}}

We read of four blessings given. We read in Genesis that Jacob blessed his sons, and blessed each with their own proper blessings. We read of the pontifical blessing: {{< verse ref="Lev 9:22" >}}And Aaron lifting up his hands towards the people, blessed them.{{< /verse >}} We read of the judicial blessing, by which Moses blessed the sons of Israel in Deuteronomy. We read of the royal blessing by which Solomon blessed: *And the king stood and blessed all the assembly of Israel.*

The Lord blessed with the pontifical, royal, and judicial blessing. Of the pontifical, in Leviticus: *I am the Lord who sanctify.* Of the judicial, the Psalm: {{< verse ref="Ps 24:9" >}}He will teach the meek his ways.{{< /verse >}} [Trans. note: the Latin reads *docebit nos Dominus vias suas*, "the Lord will teach us his ways."] He blesses us with the royal blessing because he himself is king. The Psalm: *Give ear, O thou that rulest Israel: thou that leadest Joseph like a sheep.* From him, therefore, let us seek blessing.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "17" >}}

And in what manner? For men are accustomed to go out to meet their bishop, to remove their hoods, to seek his blessing earnestly, and to bow their heads. So too we, to receive the Trinity coming to us through the office of the Church, ought to go out to meet it by passing beyond carnal things. {{< verse ref="Matt 25:6" >}}Behold the bridegroom cometh, go ye forth to meet him.{{< /verse >}} — by the putting away of carnal things. Let us remove the hoods of earthly thoughts, driving away the phantoms of such thoughts. A man ought not to veil his head with corrupt imaginings, so that he may be able to gaze clearly upon heavenly things and contemplate them. Likewise, we must humble ourselves here, for God resisteth the proud but giveth grace to the humble, according to Blessed James. Likewise we ought to seek persistently through prayer. The Psalm: *Save, O Lord, thy people: and bless thy inheritance.* And elsewhere: *May God bless us, etc.*

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "18" >}}

But what is this blessing that is to be sought from us? You should know that the blessing of the Lord is manifold, according to the four goods that proceed from him. There is the blessing of fortune, which consists in temporal goods. Proverbs: {{< verse ref="Prov 10:22" >}}The blessing of the Lord maketh rich.{{< /verse >}} And in Genesis, Jacob says to Laban: *thou knowest how I have served thee, and what thy possessions have been in my hands. The Lord hath blessed thee at my coming.* Many desire this blessing; Esau desired it.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "19" >}}

There is the blessing of nature, which consists in an abundance of natural goods — namely an abundance of good wit, strength, health, and the like. The Psalm: {{< verse ref="Ps 144:16" >}}Thou openest thy hand, and fillest with blessing every living creature.{{< /verse >}}

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "20" >}}

The third is the blessing of grace. Ecclesiasticus: {{< verse ref="Eccli 40:17" >}}The grace of God shall stand for ever.{{< /verse >}} [Trans. note: the Latin reads *gratia Dei sicut Paradisus in benedictionibus*, "the grace of God is like a Paradise in blessings"; the Douay-Rheims differs.] And well is it compared to a Paradise, for in it there is the tree of life — that is, love of the good; the tree of knowledge — that is, knowledge of the true; and a sweet river — that is, piety toward one's neighbour. And there also is the fruit-bearing tree — that is, the virtue of living and working well. The soul that possesses this blessing of grace is a Paradise; thus grace makes the soul in this manner.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "21" >}}

The fourth is the blessing of glory, which consists in an abundance of heavenly goods. Those who are in glory receive blessing from above, because they see the glory of the good; and from the abyss, because they see the punishment of the wicked. Paul to the Ephesians: {{< verse ref="Eph 1:3" >}}Who hath blessed us with spiritual blessings in heavenly places, in Christ.{{< /verse >}} Genesis: *Come in, thou blessed of the Lord; wherefore standest thou without?*

The first blessing is not to be sought, for it is the material of covetousness, and is therefore not to be much regarded, since it is the material of pride and vainglory. The second blessing of grace is greatly to be desired, for it is the material of holiness. But the blessing of glory is above all to be longed for, for it is the material of every good; there all goods are held. Let us therefore say: *May God bless us, our God, etc.*

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "22" >}}

Fourthly, by what means this blessing is to be sought: I say for the present that there are four. For there are four virtues which obtain and merit this blessing, namely innocence, mercy, obedience, and charity. For the first three, see the sermon on Blessed Benedict in the Lyons sermons on feasts.

On charity, note according to Augustine that love assimilates us to God, unites us, and informs us.

{{< /section >}}

{{< section "23" >}}

Note also that in this charity which leads to the blessing of glory there are degrees: first, ardent desire; second, sighing and lamentation; third, divine consolation; fourth, firm cleaving — and this degree merits God's blessing, which was prefigured in Jacob's wrestling with the angel. Genesis: {{< verse ref="Gen 32:26" >}}I will not let thee go except thou bless me.{{< /verse >}} And he blessed him in the same place.

{{< /section >}}
