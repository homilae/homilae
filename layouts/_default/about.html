About Homiliae

An open-access archive of Christian preaching across twenty centuries

What is this?

Homiliae is a free, open-access archive of Christian homilies and sermons delivered before 1900. Every text is presented in its original language alongside an English translation, displayed side by side so that readers can follow both at once.

The archive spans the full tradition of Christian preaching — from the Greek and Latin Fathers of the early Church, through the great medieval homiliaries, to the Counter-Reformation preachers and the bishops and priests of the modern Catholic world. Many of the texts here have never previously been available in English.

Why does this exist?

The preaching of the Church is one of the great treasures of Christian civilisation. Augustine preached almost daily for thirty years. Chrysostom moved crowds of thousands. Massillon preached at Versailles every Lent for three decades. Bourdaloue was so popular that audiences arrived hours early to secure a place.

Almost none of this is accessible in English. Academic translations exist for a handful of the most famous Fathers, locked behind expensive series or out-of-print volumes. The vast majority — medieval homiliaries, Counter-Reformation sermons, modern Catholic preaching — has never been translated at all.

This project exists to change that, one sermon at a time.

How are translations made?

All translations in this archive are produced using AI language models — specifically large language models trained on extensive corpora of classical, medieval, and early modern texts. Every translation is reviewed by the editor before publication.

We use AI translation for two reasons. First, accuracy: modern frontier AI models handle Latin, Greek, French, Spanish, Italian, and Portuguese with a level of precision and theological sensitivity that is genuinely remarkable. For patristic Latin and Counter-Reformation French in particular, AI translation consistently produces dignified, theologically aware English that respects the register and vocabulary of the original. Second, cost: professional academic translation of a single sermon can cost hundreds of pounds and take months to commission. AI makes it possible to translate an entire corpus for free, which is what allows this archive to exist at all.

Every translation is labelled as AI-assisted. These translations should not be cited in academic work without independent verification against the original. For devotional reading, catechesis, and general study, they are reliable. Errors and corrections are always welcome.

Why AI and not human translators?

The honest answer is that human translation at this scale is not possible without significant funding. The preachers in this archive — Bourdaloue, Massillon, Peregrinus of Opole, Severian of Gabala — have gone untranslated for centuries not because scholars do not value them, but because translation is expensive and publishing is slow. A single volume in an academic series can take a decade to produce and cost £80 to buy.

AI changes that equation entirely. A sermon that would take a professional translator several days and cost hundreds of pounds can be translated, reviewed, and published here in an afternoon at zero cost. The result is not a substitute for critical academic translation — it is something that did not exist before: a freely accessible English rendering of texts that were previously available only to those who read Latin or French fluently.

We believe an imperfect translation freely available to all is more valuable to the Church than a perfect translation locked behind a paywall or never produced at all.

Texts and copyright

All original-language texts in this archive are in the public domain. Every preacher represented here died before 1900 and every text was composed before 1900.

All translations are newly produced for this archive and are released under a Creative Commons Attribution licence (CC BY 4.0). You are free to use, share, and adapt them for any purpose, including commercial use, provided you attribute the source.

How to cite

When citing a homily from this archive, please use the following format:

[Author], "[Title]," trans. Homiliae Archive, Homiliae: Christian Preaching Archive, accessed [date], [URL].

For AI-assisted translations, add: "AI-assisted translation, reviewed [year]."

Corrections and contributions

This is a small project built by one person with no budget. Errors will exist. If you find a mistranslation, a wrong attribution, or a source problem, please write to:

homiliae.archive@gmail.com

If you are a Latin or Greek scholar and would like to contribute a translation or review existing ones, that help is very warmly welcomed.

The project

Homiliae is an independent, non-commercial project with the aim of making the preaching tradition of the Christian Church freely available in English. It is built with Hugo and hosted on GitHub Pages. All content is free, ad-free, and always will be.
